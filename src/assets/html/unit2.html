Physics Notes:
Acceleration, Law of Inertia, Free Fall

2.1: The Law of Inertia (Newton's First Law)

  • Statement: An object at rest stays at rest, and an object in motion stays in motion with the same speed and in the same direction unless acted upon by an unbalanced force.

  • Key Concepts:

    • Inertia: The natural tendency of an object to resist changes in its state of motion. More mass means more inertia.
    • Unbalanced Force (Net Force): A force that is not opposed by an equal and opposite force. It is required to change an object's velocity (i.e., to accelerate it).
    • Constant Velocity: Implies both constant speed and constant direction. If either changes, there's an unbalanced force acting on the object.

  • Importance:

    • Defines the concept of an **inertial reference frame** (a frame where this law holds true).
    • Foundation for understanding forces and motion; objects don't spontaneously change their motion.

  • Example: When a car suddenly brakes, passengers lurch forward due to their inertia (tendency to continue moving forward). When a car accelerates quickly, passengers are pushed back into their seats due to their inertia (tendency to resist starting motion).

2.2: Acceleration (\(\vec{a}\))

  • Definition: Acceleration is the rate at which the velocity of an object changes. It is a **vector quantity**, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.

  • Key Idea: An object accelerates if its speed changes, its direction changes, or both change.

  • Symbol: Usually denoted as \(\vec{a}\).

  • Units: Meters per second squared (\(m/s^2\)) in the SI system.

  • Formulae:

    • Average Acceleration (\(\vec{a}_{avg}\)): The change in velocity divided by the time interval over which the change occurs.

      $$\vec{a}_{avg} = \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\vec{v}_{final} - \vec{v}_{initial}}{t_{final} - t_{initial}}$$

    • Instantaneous Acceleration (\(\vec{a}\)): The acceleration of an object at a specific instant in time.

      $$\vec{a} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec{v}}{\Delta t} = \frac{d\vec{v}}{dt}$$

      (The derivative of velocity with respect to time).

  • Direction of Acceleration:

    • If an object is speeding up in the positive direction, acceleration is positive.
    • If an object is slowing down in the positive direction, acceleration is negative (often called deceleration).
    • If an object is speeding up in the negative direction, acceleration is negative.
    • If an object is slowing down in the negative direction, acceleration is positive.
    • Acceleration can also be perpendicular to velocity (e.g., in uniform circular motion), causing a change in direction without a change in speed.

2.3: Free Fall

  • Definition: Free fall is the motion of an object solely under the influence of gravity, with air resistance being negligible.

  • Key Characteristics:

    • Constant Acceleration: All objects in free fall near the Earth's surface experience the same constant downward acceleration, regardless of their mass or shape (in the absence of air resistance).
    • Acceleration due to Gravity (\(g\)): This constant acceleration is denoted by \(g\).
      • Its approximate magnitude near the Earth's surface is \(g \approx 9.8 \, m/s^2\).
      • The direction of \(g\) is always vertically downward.
    • Equations of Motion: The kinematic equations for constant acceleration can be applied to free fall, often by replacing \(a\) with \(-g\) (if upward is positive) or \(+g\) (if downward is positive).
      • \(\vec{v} = \vec{v}_0 + \vec{a}t \implies v_y = v_{0y} - gt\)
      • \(\Delta \vec{y} = \vec{v}_0 t + \frac{1}{2}\vec{a}t^2 \implies \Delta y = v_{0y}t - \frac{1}{2}gt^2\)
      • \(v^2 = v_0^2 + 2\vec{a}\Delta \vec{y} \implies v_y^2 = v_{0y}^2 - 2g\Delta y\)

  • Example: A dropped ball, a tossed coin after leaving the hand, or a skydiver before opening their parachute (ignoring air resistance). A feather and a hammer dropped in a vacuum will hit the ground at the same time.
