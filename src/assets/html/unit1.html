Physics Notes:
Reference Frames, Displacement, and Velocity

1. Reference Frames

  • Definition: A reference frame is a system for specifying the precise location of objects in space and their changes in position over time. It's essentially a coordinate system (e.g., Cartesian x-y-z axes) with an associated clock.

  • Key Idea: All measurements of position, displacement, and velocity are made *relative to a chosen reference frame*.

  • Importance:

    • Relativity of Motion: The description of motion depends on the reference frame from which it is observed. An object might appear stationary in one frame but moving in another.

      • Example: A person sitting on a train is stationary relative to the train, but moving relative to the ground.

    • Simplification: Choosing an appropriate reference frame can significantly simplify the analysis of a problem.

  • Types of Reference Frames:

    • Inertial Reference Frame:

      • A reference frame in which Newton's First Law (Law of Inertia) holds true.
      • An object at rest remains at rest, and an object in motion continues in motion with constant velocity, unless acted upon by a net external force.
      • These frames are either at rest or moving with constant velocity (no acceleration).
      • Example: A laboratory fixed to the Earth's surface (approximately), or a spaceship drifting in deep space with its engines off.

    • Non-Inertial Reference Frame:

      • A reference frame that is accelerating.
      • Newton's First Law does not directly apply without introducing "fictitious" or "inertial" forces (e.g., centrifugal force, Coriolis force).
      • Example: A rotating carousel, a car accelerating or decelerating, or a train rounding a curve.

2. Displacement (\(\Delta \vec{x}\) or \(\Delta \vec{r}\))

  • Definition: Displacement is the *change in position* of an object. It is a **vector quantity**, meaning it has both magnitude and direction.

  • Symbol: Usually denoted as \(\Delta \vec{x}\) (for motion along x-axis) or \(\Delta \vec{r}\) (for general 2D/3D motion).

  • Formula:

    $$\Delta \vec{x} = \vec{x}_{final} - \vec{x}_{initial}$$

    • \(\vec{x}_{final}\): final position vector
    • \(\vec{x}_{initial}\): initial position vector

  • Key Characteristics:

    • Vector Quantity: Always specify direction.
    • Path Independent: Displacement only depends on the initial and final positions, not on the actual path taken between them.
      • Example: Walking 5 meters north, then 5 meters south, results in a net displacement of 0 meters, even though the total distance traveled is 10 meters.
    • Units: Meters (m) in the SI system.

  • Distinction from Distance:

    • Distance: A **scalar quantity** that represents the total length of the path traveled. It is always non-negative.
    • Displacement: A **vector quantity** that represents the straight-line change in position from start to end. Can be positive, negative, or zero.
    • Analogy: If you walk around a circular track and end up at your starting point, your distance traveled is the circumference of the track, but your displacement is zero.

3. Velocity (\(\vec{v}\))

  • Definition: Velocity is the rate of change of an object's position with respect to time. It is a **vector quantity**.

  • Key Characteristics:

    • Vector Quantity: Has both magnitude (speed) and direction.
    • Units: Meters per second (m/s) in the SI system.

Types of Velocity:

  • Average Velocity (\(\vec{v}_{avg}\)):

    • Definition: The total displacement divided by the total time taken for that displacement.
    • Formula:

      $$\vec{v}_{avg} = \frac{\Delta \vec{x}}{\Delta t} = \frac{\vec{x}_{final} - \vec{x}_{initial}}{t_{final} - t_{initial}}$$

    • Key Idea: It tells you the overall, straight-line rate of motion over a period, without considering any changes in speed or direction during that period.
    • Graphical Interpretation (1D): The slope of the secant line connecting two points on a position-time graph.

  • Instantaneous Velocity (\(\vec{v}\)):

    • Definition: The velocity of an object at a specific instant in time.
    • Formula:

      $$\vec{v} = \lim_{\Delta t \to 0} \frac{\Delta \vec{x}}{\Delta t} = \frac{d\vec{x}}{dt}$$

      (This involves calculus, representing the derivative of position with respect to time).
    • Key Idea: It tells you how fast and in what direction an object is moving at a precise moment.
    • Graphical Interpretation (1D): The slope of the tangent line to the position-time graph at a specific point in time.
    • Magnitude of Instantaneous Velocity: The magnitude of instantaneous velocity is called **instantaneous speed**.

Distinction from Speed:

  • Speed: A **scalar quantity** that represents how fast an object is moving (the magnitude of velocity). It does not include direction.

    • Average Speed: Total distance traveled divided by total time.
    • Formula: Average Speed = $$\frac{\text{Total Distance}}{\text{Total Time}}$$

  • Velocity: A **vector quantity** that tells both how fast an object is moving and in what direction.

  • Example: A car traveling at 60 km/h has a speed of 60 km/h. If it's traveling at 60 km/h North, that's its velocity.
